Let us describe what we mean by resonance when damping is present. Since there were no conflicts when solving with undetermined coefficient, there is no term that goes to infinity. We look instead at the maximum value of the amplitude of the steady periodic solution. Let \(C\) be the amplitude of \(x_{sp}\text{.}\) If we plot \(C\) as a function of \(\omega\) (with all other parameters fixed), we can find its maximum. We call the \(\omega\) that achieves this maximum the practical resonance frequency. We call the maximal amplitude \(C(\omega)\) the practical resonance amplitude. Thus when damping is present we talk of practical resonance rather than pure resonance. A sample plot for three different values of \(c\) is given in FigureÂ 2.8. As you can see the practical resonance amplitude grows as damping gets smaller, and practical resonance can disappear altogether when damping is large.
