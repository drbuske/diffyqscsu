It works as follows: Take \(x_0\) and compute the slope \(k = f(x_0,y_0)\text{.}\) The slope is the change in \(y\) per unit change in \(x\text{.}\) Follow the line for an interval of length \(h\) on the \(x\)-axis. Hence if \(y = y_0\) at \(x_0\text{,}\) then we say that \(y_1\) (the approximate value of \(y\) at \(x_1 = x_0 + h\)) is \(y_1 = y_0 + h k\text{.}\) Rinse, repeat! Let \(k = f(x_1,y_1)\text{,}\) and then compute \(x_2 = x_1 + h\text{,}\) and \(y_2 = y_1 + h k\text{.}\) Now compute \(x_3\) and \(y_3\) using \(x_2\) and \(y_2\text{,}\) etc. Consider the equation \(y' = \nicefrac{y^2}{3}\text{,}\) \(y(0)=1\text{,}\) and \(h=1\text{.}\) Then \(x_0=0\) and \(y_0 = 1\text{.}\) We compute
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} & x_1 = x_0 + h = 0 + 1 = 1, & & y_1 = y_0 + h \, f(x_0,y_0) = 1 + 1 \cdot \nicefrac{1}{3} = \nicefrac{4}{3} \approx 1.333,\\ & x_2 = x_1 + h = 1 + 1 = 2, & & y_2 = y_1 + h \, f(x_1,y_1) = \nicefrac{4}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{{(\nicefrac{4}{3})}^2}{3} = \nicefrac{52}{27} \approx 1.926. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
We then draw an approximate graph of the solution by connecting the points \((x_0,y_0)\text{,}\) \((x_1,y_1)\text{,}\) \((x_2,y_2)\text{,....}\) For the first two steps of the method see Figure¬†1.16.
