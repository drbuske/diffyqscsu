In general, we want to be able to apply the Laplace transform to rational functions, that is functions of the form
\begin{equation*} \frac{F(s)}{G(s)} \end{equation*}
where \(F(s)\) and \(G(s)\) are polynomials. Since normally, for the functions that we are considering, the Laplace transform goes to zero as \(s \to \infty\text{,}\) it is not hard to see that the degree of \(F(s)\) must be smaller than that of \(G(s)\text{.}\) Such rational functions are called proper rational functions and we can always apply the method of partial fractions. Of course this means we need to be able to factor the denominator into linear and quadratic terms, which involves finding the roots of the denominator.
